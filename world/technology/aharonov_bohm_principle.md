# The Aharonov-Bohm Principle in StillPoint Physics

## The Real Science

In 1959, physicists David Bohm and Yakir Aharonov proposed something heretical: the invisible mathematical scaffolding of physics — called *potentials* — might be more fundamental than the forces we can actually measure.

Their thought experiment: fire two beams of electrons around a solenoid (a tightly wound coil of wire). The magnetic field is confined entirely inside the coil. Outside, where the electrons travel, the field is exactly zero. But the magnetic *potential* — a quantity most physicists dismissed as a mere calculation trick — extends into the space around the solenoid.

Aharonov and Bohm predicted that even with no field present, the potential alone would shift the interference pattern of the electrons. Most physicists thought this was impossible. Niels Bohr himself rejected it.

In 1986, Akira Tonomura's team at Hitachi settled the debate. They used a tiny donut-shaped magnet (a torus) coated in superconducting niobium — a perfect seal that guaranteed zero field leakage. The interference fringes inside the donut shifted by exactly half a phase compared to outside. Peaks became troughs. Exactly as predicted.

The potential — the thing everyone had dismissed as mathematical bookkeeping — was shaping physical reality in a region where no force existed.

**Three competing interpretations remain unresolved:**
1. Potentials are physically real and more fundamental than fields
2. Fields act non-locally — influencing regions where they don't exist
3. Particles explore all possible paths simultaneously (Feynman path integrals), and some paths pass through the field region even when the "classical" path doesn't

Richard Feynman wrote of the vector potential: "A is as real as B — realer, whatever that means."
