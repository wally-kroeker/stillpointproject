# Location Card: Hawthorn Commons

## The Origins of Hawthorn Commons

Long before Hawthorn Commons was a mosaic of small, self-organizing communities, it began with a single bold act of reclamation: the transformation of an old Minneapolis megachurch.

For decades, the colossal building had towered over its neighborhood—a monument to vanished certainty, its once modern elaborate glass windows now flickering with dust and sunlight. As the Near-Future Crisis deepened and the world grew restless and hungry, the old church became a place of rumors and empty pews, a relic more than a refuge.

All that changed with the arrival of the StillPoint. Carried in by a handful of burnt-out neighbors and quietly defiant elders, the device—gentle, luminous, humming with presence—was placed on the altar, where sermons once thundered about heaven and hell. In its calm, silent orbit, something shifted: the megachurch shed its doctrine, and people returned, not for dogma, but for food, warmth, and honest company.

Soup kitchens replaced sermons. Meditation circles and “resting hours” replaced guilt and exhortation. The new “Resting Place” became a sanctuary for everyone—regardless of belief, background, or burnout. Here, real “good news” wasn’t shouted from the pulpit, but served in bowls, offered in quiet companionship, and discovered in the steady rhythms of shared meals and mutual care.

Word spread. Resting Place drew in travelers, wanderers, and organizers—people with ideas for living better, not just surviving. Small groups splintered off, seeding their own experiments in the empty lots and strip malls nearby: some gardens, some workshops, some loud and lively, others contemplative and hushed.

Over the years, these groups wove themselves into a patchwork, each commons bringing its own values and customs, but all orbiting the gravity of that first sanctuary. They called the area Hawthorn Commons—a name drawn from the hardy trees that took root along the parking lot, blooming pink in the spring, tangled and resilient in every season.

Even now, generations later, the old megachurch—its pews replaced by sleeping mats and its stage lined with solar ovens—remains the heart of it all: the Minneapolis StillPoint and Resting Place, where anyone can find food, comfort, and a different kind of salvation.
