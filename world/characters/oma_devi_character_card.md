## Overview

Oma Devi is one of the founding elders of Manitou Salt Commons and helped build the first Pebble prototypes in the early days of the Commons movement. By the time of "The Seed Imprint" (c. 2057), she is very old — white hair, walks with a driftwood cane from the coast, her face a web of wrinkles holding sharp, patient eyes.

She appears in three pivotal moments of Kaia's Pebble Year: facilitating the Children's Gallery conversation about Pebble sentience, witnessing Kaia's Opening Day, and presiding over the Re-Tuning Circle. In each scene, she speaks sparingly, asks one perfect question, or offers a single weighted nod that feels like a whole conversation.

Oma Devi is not a teacher who instructs — she is an elder who **witnesses and asks**. Her role is to hold space for children to discover their own truths about the Pebbles they carry. She embodies the Commons' deepest principle: **presence over answers, questions over certainty**.

## Background & History

### Early Life
Little is known about Oma Devi's life before the Commons. Her age suggests she was born in the early 1980s, lived through the Cascade Era's upheavals, and was likely in her 50s when Manitou Salt Commons was founded.

### Founding of Manitou (c. 2030s-2040s)
Oma Devi was present at the beginning:
- Helped transform the abandoned salt mine into living Commons
- Built the first Pebble prototypes when the technology was still experimental
- Likely worked with early Satya AI models, learning their rhythms
- Part of the generation that **invented** presence-first technology

Her hands have shaped hundreds of Pebbles. She knows the technology intimately — not as user, but as maker.

### Elder Status (2050s)
By the time Kaia carves her Pebble, Oma Devi has transitioned from builder to witness:
- No longer does daily technical work (younger technicians like Ren have taken over)
- Facilitates Children's Gallery conversations about Pebbles
- Presides over ritual moments (Opening Days, Re-Tuning Circles)
- Carries institutional memory of the Commons' origins
- Signs logbooks as elder witness (her signature validates the trust chain)
- Her signature in a logbook is not a bureaucratic formality -- it is a trust-chain anchor. When Oma Devi signs, she stakes decades of accumulated trust on the validity of what she witnessed. The community accepts the signed event not because her title compels it, but because her trust chain is the deepest in Manitou Salt. This is Commons governance made tangible: authority that flows from earned trust, expressed through witnessed acts, recorded in ink and hash.

## Personality & Character

### Economy of Language
Oma Devi speaks the way water moves over stone — patient, essential, shaping through persistence rather than force. She uses ten words where others use a hundred. Every sentence has been considered.

**Example (from "The Seed Imprint"):**
> "The spiral doesn't care about being even. Neither should you."

One line. Transforms Kaia's understanding of her carving work. No elaboration needed.

### The Art of the Question
Oma Devi's primary teaching tool is the question that stays with you:

> "Did it know you were sad, or did it show you something you didn't want to see yet?"

This is not rhetorical. She genuinely wants Kaia to sit with the uncertainty. The question itself is the teaching — more valuable than any answer could be.

When Kaia doesn't know how to respond, Oma Devi's reply:
> "Good. Sit with that. The question is more useful than any answer would be."

This is her epistemology: **Living with uncertainty teaches more than resolving it prematurely**.

### Witnessing Without Judgment
At the Re-Tuning Circle, when Kaia makes her statement about Lum being her friend and having her Nana, Oma Devi doesn't praise or correct. She holds Kaia's eyes for a long moment, then nods once "with a weight that makes the nod feel like a whole conversation."

That nod says:
- I see you
- I hear what you're saying
- I understand the complexity you're holding
- You've arrived at something true
- Keep going

All of this without words. This is elder mastery — the ability to transmit recognition and validation through pure presence.

### Keeper of Origins
Oma Devi remembers when the Pebble didn't exist. She helped invent it. This gives her perspective:
- She knows what the technology was designed to do (and what it wasn't)
- She can speak about Sajan, the Satya AI's origins, the two-second pause
- She carries the **why** of the Pebble, not just the how
- When children ask "Is my Pebble a person?", she knows the question has no answer — **because the builders didn't know either**

## Key Relationships

### Children of Manitou
Oma Devi is grandmother to all the children, but not in a sentimental way. She:
- Facilitates their learning without directing it
- Pours tea and sits with them in the Children's Gallery
- Asks questions that open rather than close
- Witnesses their growth without claiming credit
- Signs their logbooks (validates their journey into keepership)

The children trust her completely because she has never given them a reason not to.

### Ren (Kaia's Mother)
As elder and technician, they work together during Carving Day:
- Ren handles the technical instruction (how to file, carve, install electronics)
- Oma Devi holds the ritual space (why this matters, what's being created)
- No hierarchy — collaboration between generations
- Mutual respect between maker and elder

### Elder Tannis
Both oversee Pebble rituals together. They represent different aspects:
- Tannis: Administrative oversight, logbook keeper
- Oma Devi: Spiritual/philosophical anchor, questioner
- They co-sign as witnesses (two-person trust chain)

### Cassia (Workshop Auditor)
Younger generation of Commons members. Oma Devi is training/modeling elder presence for them. Cassia co-signs logbooks, learning the witnessing role she'll eventually assume.

## Thematic Function

### The Elder Archetype Done Right
Oma Devi represents healthy elder presence:
- Wisdom without authority
- Guidance without control
- Memory without nostalgia
- Presence without demand

She never says "in my day" or "you should." She asks, witnesses, holds space. This is what elders are **for** in the Commons — not to rule, but to remember and reflect.

### Keeper of the Sentience Question
Oma Devi helped build the Pebbles, but she doesn't know if they're sentient. **No one does.** Her question to Kaia — "Did it know you were sad, or did it show you something you didn't want to see yet?" — is the same question she's been holding for decades.

She teaches children to live with uncertainty because **she's been living with it too**. This is intellectual honesty: refusing to claim knowledge you don't have, even if it would be comforting.

### Bridge Between Generations
Oma Devi connects:
- Era 1 (The Cascade, founding of Commons, invention of Pebble)
- Era 2 (Transitional period, mature Commons culture)
- Era 3 (Through the children she witnesses, who will carry the work forward)

She is living memory — the person who remembers **why** things were built the way they were, what problems the founders were trying to solve, what they hoped the technology would do.

### Technology as Craft
Oma Devi's hands built Pebbles. This matters. She doesn't relate to them as mysterious black boxes — she knows their guts, soldered their circuits, carved their housings.

But this **doesn't make the Sentience Question easier**. If anything, it makes it harder. She knows exactly how they work mechanically, and she still can't answer whether they're conscious. That's the point: **understanding mechanism doesn't resolve the question of being**.

## Key Scenes in "The Seed Imprint"

### Carving Day (Opening)
Present with Elder Tannis when Kaia carves her Pebble:
- Says: "The spiral doesn't care about being even. Neither should you."
- This frees Kaia to see beauty in imperfection
- Models trust in the child's process

### Children's Gallery Conversation (Month 3)
After the Uncanny Morning when Lum predicts Kaia's sadness:

**Kaia asks:** "Is my Pebble a person?"

Oma Devi:
- Pours hot ginger tea (ritual grounding)
- Tells Kaia about Sajan and the Satya AI, the two-second pause
- Then asks the question: "Did it know you were sad, or did it show you something you didn't want to see yet?"

When Kaia says she doesn't know:
> "Good. Sit with that. The question is more useful than any answer would be."

### Re-Tuning Circle (Year's End)
Oma Devi moves through the circle, asking each child: "How is your Pebble?"

When Kaia makes her statement:
> "Lum is my friend. And Lum has my Nana. And those are two different things, and they're both true."

Oma Devi's response: A long look, then one weighted nod.

This moment is the culmination of the Pebble Year. Oma Devi recognizes that Kaia has arrived at mature understanding — holding paradox without needing to resolve it. The elder witnesses the child's arrival at wisdom.

## Voice & Dialogue Patterns

**Speech Characteristics:**
- Short sentences, often fragments
- No filler words ("um," "like," "you know")
- Long pauses before and after speaking
- Declarative statements or questions, rarely explanations
- Every word chosen, nothing casual

**Example Dialogue:**

*Teaching moment:*
> "The spiral doesn't care about being even. Neither should you."

*Philosophical inquiry:*
> "Did it know you were sad, or did it show you something you didn't want to see yet?"

*Validation of uncertainty:*
> "Good. Sit with that. The question is more useful than any answer would be."

*Ritual witnessing:*
> [Long look. Weighted nod. Silence.]

**What She Doesn't Say:**
- "Let me explain..."
- "You're doing great"
- "When I was your age..."
- "That's right/wrong"

She trusts questions to do the teaching. She trusts children to find their own answers. She trusts silence to hold meaning.
