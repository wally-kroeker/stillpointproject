# Lore Card: Infinite and Finite Games

**Overview:** A core philosophical framework within the StillPoint Philosophy, this concept differentiates between two opposing ways of engaging with life, society, and self. The societal shift from a finite to an infinite game mindset is the central transformation of the StillPoint saga. The concept is heavily influenced by the work of philosopher James P. Carse.

---

### The Finite Game

A finite game is played for the purpose of winning. It has a definite beginning, middle, and end, with specific rules and agreed-upon objectives. The players are known, and the outcome is clear: one person or group wins, and the other loses.

*   **Mindset:** Characterized by competition, individualism, and the pursuit of external validation (e.g., power, status, wealth). It is fueled by a "separate-self narrative" where individuals see themselves as isolated actors in a struggle for limited resources.
*   **Goal:** To end the game by achieving victory.
*   **Historical Context:** The dominant mindset of the pre-Cascade world and the driving force behind the social, economic, and ecological crises of The Cascade. It prioritized short-term, measurable "wins" at the expense of long-term well-being and relational health. Loria's early career as a political strategist is a prime example of a finite game player.

### The Infinite Game

An infinite game is played for the purpose of continuing the play. There are no winners or losers, only players who continue to participate. The rules are changeable, and the goal is not to win, but to keep the game going for as long as possible.

*   **Mindset:** Characterized by cooperation, collaboration, and a focus on collective flourishing. It is rooted in an "intra-connected" identity (MWe), where the well-being of the individual is inseparable from the well-being of the whole.
*   **Goal:** To perpetuate the game, fostering resilience, creativity, and connection.
*   **Historical Context:** The guiding principle of the StillPoint movement and the Commons communities like Hawthorn Commons. It prioritizes "presence over production" and "collective well-being over individual gain." The ultimate aim is to create a society where all humans can engage in continuous, adaptive, and beneficial "play." Sajan's decision to open-source the StillPoint technology is a foundational act of infinite play.

The transition from a finite to an infinite game is not a simple choice but a profound shift in consciousness, moving from a life of striving and suffering to one of flourishing and integrated presence.
