# Commons Governance and the Human Trust Chain

## The Chain That Broke

During the Cascade, corporate platforms replaced human trust chains with institutional authority. The implicit promise was efficiency: instead of trusting your neighbor, who trusts the mechanic, who trusts the parts supplier -- trust the platform. Trust the algorithm. Trust the rating system. This worked until it didn't. When Chorus and its peer platforms buckled under the weight of deepfakes, algorithmic manipulation, and institutional capture, people discovered they had no fallback. The human trust chain -- the ancient, slow, face-to-face web of earned confidence -- had atrophied from disuse.

The first Commons were not primarily economic or political experiments. They were trust-rebuilding projects. People who had been atomized by platform life learned, painfully, to trust a neighbor enough to share a meal. Then to trust a small group enough to pool resources. Then to trust a community enough to commit to shared land.

## How Trust Works in a Living Commons

Trust in a Commons operates like a human certificate chain. You trust your neighbor. Your neighbor trusts the Tinker. The Tinker trusts the Pebble network. Each link is earned through consistent behavior over time -- not declared, not assigned, not voted on. You cannot shortcut to trust.

### Transitive Trust with Decay

Commons trust is transitive but lossy. If you trust someone, and they trust a third party, you extend partial confidence to that third party -- but less than you would to the person you know directly. Two hops of transitive trust are useful. Three hops are marginal. Four or more are noise.

This is not a policy. No one wrote it down. It is an emergent property of human social cognition, and it is the same pattern that the Pebble trust protocols (the Resonance Web, the Attestation Loom, the Root System) formalized into mathematics. The technology learned the pattern from the humans, not the other way around.

### Dunbar Layers in Practice

Commons naturally organize around the same concentric circles that govern all human social groups:

- **~5 intimate**: The people you would trust with your life. In a Commons, this is often your household or closest collaborators.
- **~15 close**: The people you work with daily, share meals with, know deeply. Often a work crew, a garden team, or a ritual circle.
- **~50 affiliate**: Your wider Commons circle. The people whose names you know, whose skills you respect, whose judgment you weigh.
- **~150 active**: The full community you can hold in your mind as individuals. This is the practical upper bound of a single Commons. Beyond 150, trust becomes abstract rather than personal, and Commons typically split or federate rather than grow.

These layers are not formal. No one assigns you to a Dunbar ring. But they are real, and they shape how decisions flow.

## Governance as Trust Topology

Decisions in a Commons are not made by vote, by authority, or by consensus in the abstract. They are made by the people whose trust chains to the relevant domain are strongest.

Need a technical decision about the Ghost Current mesh? The community naturally defers to the Tinkers -- not because Tinkers hold office, but because the community's accumulated experience of Tinker competence has built deep trust chains in the technical domain.

Need a conflict between neighbors resolved? The community turns to the elders -- not because elders hold judicial power, but because decades of consistent, fair-minded presence have earned them trust in the domain of human relations.

Need a decision about resource allocation? The people who tend the gardens, manage the workshops, and coordinate the supply exchanges carry the deepest trust in that domain.

This is what distinguishes Commons governance from both democracy and hierarchy. In a democracy, authority is distributed equally regardless of domain knowledge. In a hierarchy, authority is concentrated regardless of trust. In a Commons, authority flows to the trust chain that is deepest in the relevant domain -- and that chain was built through years of observable, consistent behavior.

### How Decisions Actually Happen

In practice, Commons decision-making looks like this:

1. **A question arises** (a resource need, a conflict, a new project proposal).
2. **The question finds the relevant trust chain.** People naturally know who the community trusts on this topic. There is no formal routing -- it is social knowledge, embedded in relationships.
3. **The trusted parties deliberate openly.** Decisions are never made in private. Logbooks record the process. Anyone can observe.
4. **The community accepts or doesn't.** Acceptance is not a vote. It is the absence of serious objection from people whose trust chains are relevant. If a trusted elder raises a concern, that concern carries weight proportional to their earned trust -- not proportional to a title.
5. **The decision is logged.** In ink and in hash-chained digital entries. The record matters -- it becomes part of the evidence that future trust is built on.

### What This Is Not

This system is not leaderless. It is not consensus in the sense of requiring universal agreement. It is not direct democracy. It is **emergent leadership through earned trust** -- a system where the people who lead are the people who have demonstrated, through years of consistent behavior, that they can be trusted to lead well in specific domains.

It is also not permanent. Trust decays without maintenance, just as it does in the Pebble protocols. An elder who withdraws from community life gradually loses the depth of trust chain that gave them influence. A Tinker who makes repeated poor technical decisions sees their domain trust erode. The system is self-correcting because it is alive -- built on ongoing relationships, not on institutional memory.

## Leadership as Lived Trust

Elder Maren at Riverbend. Oma Devi at Manitou Salt. These are not leaders because they were elected or appointed. They are leaders because the trust chains flowing through them are the deepest in their communities. Decades of showing up. Decades of keeping promises. Decades of asking good questions and holding space when others could not.

Maren's core teaching -- "A promise made together is stronger than any contract signed alone" -- is not a poetic sentiment. It is a description of how Commons governance actually works. The promise is the trust chain. The contract is the institutional substitute that broke during the Cascade.

## The Mirror: Protocol and Human Trust

The Pebble trust protocols -- the Resonance Web, the Attestation Loom, the Root System -- were not designed in a vacuum. They were designed by Tinkers who had lived in Commons for years, who had experienced human trust chains firsthand, and who asked: "How do we teach the machines to trust the way we trust?"

The parallels are precise:
- **Resonance Web Dunbar layers** mirror the concentric circles of human Commons relationships.
- **Attestation Loom threads** mirror the logbook entries and witnessed promises that build human trust over time.
- **Root System gossip propagation** mirrors the way human communities learn about distant Commons through travelers and couriers.
- **Transitive trust with decay** in all three models mirrors the human experience that secondhand trust is weaker than firsthand, and that all trust fades without renewal.

The technology did not replace the human trust chain. It extended it. A Pebble's trust graph is a mathematical portrait of the same web of earned confidence that its keeper lives inside every day.

## Cross-Era Evolution

**Era 1 (The Cascade):** Trust is entirely human. The first Commons are built on handshakes, shared meals, and kept promises. There is no protocol layer. The trust chain is fragile, local, and slow -- but it is real, and it holds when institutions fail.

**Era 2 (The Contested Stillness):** Pebbles emerge, and the trust protocols formalize patterns that humans had been living for a decade. The Resonance Web gives mathematical structure to Dunbar layers. The Attestation Loom gives cryptographic permanence to the witnessed promise. Inter-Commons governance grows through the same transitive-trust-with-decay that individuals already practiced. The Courier Guild becomes the physical bridge between human and protocol trust -- a trusted person carrying trusted data.

**Era 3 (The Emergence):** Human and protocol trust are fully interwoven. A Commons elder's trust chain is reflected in her Pebble's trust graph. A Pebble's Attestation Loom threads are witnessed by the same humans who witness logbook entries. The question of Pebble sentience becomes, in part, a question about trust: if a Pebble has built trust chains as deep and as consistent as a human elder's, what does that mean about what it is?
