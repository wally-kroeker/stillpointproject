# The Neural Stream

The neural interface warmed against Marcus Chen's temple as he made final adjustments to the calibration settings, its gentle pressure a familiar weight from eight months of verified human streaming. The device looked deceptively simple—a curved band of brushed titanium that rested against his left temple, connected by a gossamer-thin cable to the main processing unit humming quietly beneath his desk. But inside that titanium curve lay an intricate array of biosensors and neuromorphic processors that represented the bleeding edge of consciousness-sharing technology.

Unlike the crude biometric monitoring that [[Chorus]] AI used for productivity surveillance, Marcus's neural interface employed what its designers called "quantum coherence detection." The system used photonic sensors to measure minute electromagnetic fluctuations in his neural tissue—not the gross electrical patterns that traditional EEG captured, but the subtle quantum field effects that emerged when consciousness achieved certain states of clarity or stress. These patterns were then translated through specialized neuromorphic chips into data streams that his audience could experience as emotional resonance rather than mere observation.

The technology was experimental, glitchy, and expensive—which was exactly why it commanded premium prices in the 2037 attention economy. While AI systems could generate perfect analytical content, they couldn't replicate the authentic vulnerability of a human nervous system processing information in real-time. Marcus's subscribers paid not just for his insights, but for the privilege of feeling his discoveries alongside him, experiencing the texture of human curiosity as it unfolded through the neural interface.

Six monitors cast their blue glow across his cramped Minneapolis apartment, each displaying a different facet of his digital ecosystem. The Chorus Streams interface dominated the center monitor—Chorus's proprietary streaming platform, naturally, since his $800/month neural interface already locked him into their ecosystem. His face in the preview window alongside chat integration, donation notifications, viewer metrics, and the ever-present Coherence Score indicator that determined how the algorithm surfaced his content to potential subscribers. To his left: research databases from three different economic institutes, social media monitoring tools, and a government data portal he'd maintained access to since his trading days. To his right: his neural feedback dashboard displaying brainwave patterns, stress indicators, and emotional resonance metrics; subscriber demographics and engagement analytics; and a backup recording system that captured everything in case the primary stream failed.

The soft hum of cooling fans provided a constant backdrop, punctuated by the mechanical click of his keyboard—a vintage model from his cryptocurrency trading days when milliseconds meant fortunes and the tactile feedback of Cherry MX switches gave him confidence in his rapid-fire buy and sell decisions. Those days felt like a lifetime ago, though only eight years had passed since the 2028 wage collapse had wiped out his portfolio and forced him to rebuild his life from the ground up.

That rebuild remained precarious. Two months behind on rent in this cramped apartment. The neural interface cost $800 monthly just to rent—non-negotiable for verified human status.
