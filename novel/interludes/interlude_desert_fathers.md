ERA: 4th-Century Scetis Desert (ca. 370 CE)
LOCATION: Wadi el-Natrun, Egypt — a thorn-tree outside Abba Serapion’s cell
POV CHARACTER(S): Makarios the Scribe (novice pilgrim)
VOICE/TENSE: V-Interlude

---

Dust sighs across the limestone flats as dawn thins the night’s indigo. Makarios walks barefoot, carrying nothing but a water skin and a scrap of parchment tucked into his belt. Twenty days ago, in Alexandria’s tangled alleys, a spice merchant told him of hermits in the western wasteland who “speak with God by saying nothing.” The phrase has haunted him through every footsore mile.

He crests a low dune. Below, a cluster of mud-brick cells huddles like beggars against a lone thorn-tree. No doorways face outward; all openings turn to the tree, as if the brothers fear their own gaze more than the desert. Smoke lifts from a single clay chimney, vanishing before it can name itself.

Makarios descends, heart drumming. At the tree’s base sits Abba Serapion—thin, weather-scored, his beard salt-white against skin dark as baked earth. A rope of knotted cord coils through his fingers, lips moving in silent measure. Each knot pinched equals one measured breath; when the cord completes its loop, an hour will have slipped away.

Makarios clears his throat. “Father, I come seeking a word.”

Serapion’s eyes open, patient as old stone. He gestures to the dust beside him. They sit. The cotton of Makarios’s tunic clings damp to his spine; the desert is already warm, and the sun not yet full. The only sound is wind whispering through brittle thorns.

Minutes pass—five, ten, perhaps thirty; Makarios cannot tell. He feels each heartbeat like a hammer and wants to speak again, to explain his sins, his failures, the city’s clamor echoing inside his skull. But each time the urge rises, Serapion’s stillness meets it the way a pool meets a stone: accepting the plunk, then swallowing all ripples.

A camel bell rings faintly on the horizon; a vulture wheels overhead. Otherwise, the world purrs in silence.

Finally Serapion breathes out a single sentence, voice low as wind in a jar:

“The mouth that tastes honey does not speak of honey.”

Makarios blinks. “I… do not understand.”

Another long hush. Serapion runs the cord across his palm, knot to knot. When he speaks again, it is a question: “What flavor has silence?”

Makarios gropes. “No flavor… and yet everything tastes sharper after.”

Serapion’s eyes soften—approval, maybe. “Stay until sunset. Taste it fully.”

He rises, enters his cell, and leaves Makarios under the thorn. The novice stares at the knots still swaying from the elder’s retreating hand. He feels abandoned, yet strangely invited.

### Midday

Sun climbs, erasing shadows. Heat shimmers across the flats; lizards dart between stones. Makarios’s water dwindles. Thirst prickles, but pride tethers him in place. He recalls city fountains, the chatter of scholars debating the Trinity, the smell of frying fish; each memory shrivels in the glare.

Flies worry his eyelids. Sweat salts his lips. Breathing becomes audible, ragged. In the distance, he hears a brother chanting psalms within mud walls—low droning syllables slurred by heat. The chant falters, fades. Silence again.

Makarios closes his eyes, counting breaths against the memory of Serapion’s rope. Ten breaths. Twenty. Thoughts crowd—“Why did I come? Who will copy the scrolls if I stay?”—then tire and thin, like merchants leaving a festival once lamps are doused.

Presently, something shifts: the ache in his knees loosens; the sun’s hammer softens to quilt. He hears, unmistakably, his own pulse slowing. The thorn-tree’s scant shade feels cool as Nile water. Time unspools; wind stroking thorns becomes a hymn.

### Late Afternoon

A cedar bowl appears beside him—dates, a scoop of barley, a thumb-sized clay cup holding cloudy water. Makarios never saw who placed it. He eats slowly, each date fleshy with sweetness beyond markets. The barley tastes of stone and sun, yet feeds him more than bread ever did. The water is warm, but when it slides down his throat, gratitude flowers behind his ribs.

He does not look for Serapion. He suspects the elder watches from shadowed doorway, or perhaps not at all. The desert itself seems witness enough.

### Sunset

When the horizon flames gold, Serapion returns, cord looped anew. He sits without greeting; Makarios mirrors him. Together they watch the sun’s disc melt into dunefall. Air cools; color drains. In that hush Makarios feels an unfamiliar courage, like the steady arm of an invisible friend.

Serapion speaks once more: “What flavor has silence, little brother?”

Makarios answers without thought. “It is a sweetness that asks nothing of the tongue.”

Serapion nods. “Then carry that flavor back to Alexandria, and let your deeds speak.”

He stands, knotted cord dangling like a tame serpent, and pads toward the cells. Stars prick the velvet sky. Makarios rises, legs numb but spirit bright, and bows—not to the elder, but to the stillness that bound them.

### Epilogue Moment

He will walk east at dawn, scrolls awaiting his copyist’s hand. Yet something fundamental has shifted: the city’s arguments will sound like distant thunder; the sweetness will linger on his palate. In crowded marketplaces, he will pause mid-stride and taste the silent flavor, and others, seeing the softness in his gaze, may ask for a word.

Perhaps he will answer as Serapion did—with a question. Or perhaps he will simply breathe, knot to knot, until their own stillness answers for them.
