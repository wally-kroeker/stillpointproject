ERA: Autumn 1538 · English Midlands
LOCATION: Priory of St. Augustine, a small Augustinian house near Warwickshire
POV CHARACTER(S): Brother Anselm (cellarer)
VOICE/TENSE: V-Interlude

---

*From the Logbooks of Loria:*
*The silence we choose is prayer. The silence imposed on us is something else entirely — a wound in the shape of what was taken. Learn to tell the difference. One heals. The other waits.*

---

The bread is wrong.

Brother Anselm knows it before his hands touch the dough — too slack, too wet, the leaven half-hearted in the cold. October has come early this year, and the oven draws poorly when the wind sits northeast. He folds and turns, folds and turns, pressing his weight through the heels of his palms while the bell for Prime shivers the dark.

He leaves the dough to prove. Wipes flour on his scapular. Crosses the cloister garth where frost silvers the rosemary, and the air smells of stone and leaf-rot and something older — three centuries of feet wearing the same path between dorter and choir. His sandals know the flagstones by feel. Third from the corner: cracked. Fifth: slightly raised where a root has worked beneath. He could walk it blind.

In the choir, twelve voices begin the psalms. Thirteen, if you count Brother Thomas, whose throat has thickened with a winter cough that will not leave and who mouths the words more than sings them. The sound is thin. It was always thin — this is no great abbey with forty monks and an organ, but a small house, a house of bread and bandages and boys learning their letters. The sound is thin, and it is enough. It has been enough since the first Augustinians laid stone here in the reign of the second Edward.

*Deus, in adiutorium meum intende.*

Anselm's lips move. His mind catalogues: the barley store is low, the infirmary needs tallow for poultices, the widow Margery's rent is three weeks overdue but her son has the flux and he will not press her. This is what a cellarer does. He holds the weight of bread and debt and leaking roofs so that the others may hold the weight of prayer. He has never resented it. God made him practical. The prior says this as though it were a lesser gift, but the prior does not know where the candles come from.

After Prime, the hours unspool as they always have. Terce. A lesson in the scriptorium for the village boys — Brother William's chalk scratching on the slate, patient as rain. Sext. Anselm in the garden pulling the last of the cabbages, earth cold and friable under his nails. The infirmary brother dressing old Martin's leg, the ulcer that will not close. None. The gate, where Agnes and her daughter collect the day's alms — a half-loaf, a cup of pottage, a blessing muttered through the grille.

These ordinary things.

He does not know, yet, that he is living the last day of them.

---

The rider comes at None, while Anselm is weighing cheese in the buttery. Brother Peter finds him — white-faced, his stammer worse than usual.

"C-Commissioners. Tomorrow. With the Act."

Anselm sets down the cheese. The wheel of it, pale and sweating on the board, suddenly looks obscene in its completeness. He has been aging it since Lammastide. It will never be eaten here.

He already knew, of course. They all knew. The smaller houses fell two years ago under the first Act of Suppression — any house with an income under two hundred pounds. Their priory had scraped above that line, barely. But Cromwell's men have new instructions now. The second Act makes no such distinction. Everything falls.

"Who brought word?"

"F-farmer Colley. He saw them on the Coventry road. Six men. A cart."

A cart. For the plate, the vestments, the lead from the roof. Anselm has heard what happens: the Commissioners inventory everything, strip what has value, pension off the monks — four pounds a year if you are fortunate, nothing if you are not — and hand the buildings to whatever gentleman or wool merchant has bought the grant. Within a season, sheep graze where the cloister was.

He steadies himself against the buttery wall. The stone is cool, slightly damp. He can feel, through his fingertips, three hundred years of cellarers leaning here with the same weariness after the same long days.

"Tell the prior," he says. "I will see to things."

But what things? What is there to see to? The silver belongs to the King. The books belong to the King. The land, the buildings, the bell — all the King's now, or will be by this time tomorrow. He cannot steal. He will not steal. That is not the kind of man God made him.

He goes back to the bread.

---

Vespers, and the light fails early. Tallow candles gutter in the choir — beeswax is for feast days, and this is no feast, though it is an ending. The prior's voice cracks on the Magnificat. No one pretends not to notice. Brother William, who is sixty and has lived in this house since he was given to it at seven years old, weeps without sound, tears catching in the deep lines of his face like rain in plough-furrows.

Anselm watches them. His brothers. Thomas with his cough. Peter with his stammer. Young Simon, who came last year fleeing something he will not name and who has only just begun to sleep through the night. William, who can illuminate a capital letter so fine it seems to breathe. The prior, who is vain and fussy and has governed this small house with a gentleness that will be mistaken, by the Commissioners, for weakness.

He loves them. He has not thought this before in so many words. The word seems too large for what they share — this daily rhythm of bread and prayer and mending and silence. But there it is, clumsy and true, filling his chest like the ache before weeping.

After Vespers, the prior gathers them in the chapter house. His voice is steady now, the crack mended with effort.

"We shall sing Compline tonight as we have always sung it. We shall sing it well."

No one asks what comes after.

---

Compline.

The Great Silence begins, and this time it will not end.

Twelve men file into the choir for the last time. The candles have been lit — all of them, beeswax and tallow both, an extravagance that no cellarer would permit under ordinary circumstances. But Anselm lit them himself, every one, and the prior said nothing.

The choir glows. Stone arches hold the light the way a cupped hand holds water — briefly, preciously, already losing it.

*Te lucis ante terminum* — the hymn before sleep, before darkness, before the end of the day and the world and everything they have known. The voices find each other. Thin, yes. Ragged where Thomas coughs and thin where Simon's tenor wavers. But together, woven, a fabric of breath and pitch that has covered this house every night for three centuries. The melody is older than the building. Older than the order. It reaches back through Augustine to the desert, to the first monks who marked the hours because marking the hours was how you remembered that time belonged to God and not to you.

Anselm sings. His voice is a serviceable bass, nothing more. But tonight it carries something he cannot name — a weight, a sweetness, an anger that has nowhere to go.

*Procul recedant somnia, et noctium phantasmata.*

Let dreams withdraw, and the phantoms of the night.

The phantoms are real this time. Six men on the Coventry road with a cart and an inventory.

The psalm ends. Silence. Not the silence of rest — the silence before a blow.

The prior speaks the blessing. *Noctem quietam et finem perfectum concedat nobis Dominus omnipotens.*

A quiet night and a perfect end.

They file out. No one speaks. The Great Silence holds, and within it, Anselm hears each man's breathing — quick, slow, ragged, steady — as though listening for the last time to a music he had never thought to memorise.

---

He does not go to the dorter.

Instead, he returns to the choir. Empty now. The candles still burn — he will let them. What is the point of thrift when the house itself will be sold for its lead and timber?

He sits in his stall. The wood is smooth where three centuries of woollen habits have polished it. He runs his thumb along the misericord — the small carved shelf beneath the seat, meant to support a man through the long hours of standing prayer. This one is carved with a fox stealing a goose. A jest, hidden where only the sitter would know. Some brother, two hundred years dead, who had a sense of humour about the hours.

Anselm breathes.

He is not a contemplative. He has said this many times, to the prior, to the novices, to himself. He is the bread man, the cheese man, the man who counts pennies and argues with merchants and knows the price of tallow in three counties. Prayer is his duty, not his gift.

But sitting here, in the guttering light, with the smell of beeswax and cold stone and the ghost of twelve voices still humming in the vaulting, he understands something he has missed for twenty-six years of the Daily Office: the prayer was never his. It was never any single monk's. It was the house praying. The hours praying through them. The rhythm itself — Lauds to Compline, dark to dark — was the prayer, and they were merely the breath it used.

And tomorrow the breath stops.

He rises. Goes to the small chest in the vestry where the horologium is kept — the book that governs the Office, that tells which psalms for which hours, which antiphons for which feasts, the entire architecture of sacred time laid out in careful hand. It is not valuable. Not like the chalice or the silver pyx that the Commissioners will weigh and record. It is a working book, dog-eared, stained with candle-wax, the binding cracked. No one will inventory it. No one will miss it.

He wraps it in sacking cloth. Carries it across the cloister to the gate. The night is clear and bitter. Stars like scattered salt.

He leaves it beneath the stone where Agnes collects the alms. She will come at dawn, as she always comes. She cannot read — but her daughter can. The girl has sharp eyes, a quick mind. Brother William taught her letters last winter when she brought her mother to the gate, and she learned faster than any of the village boys.

It is not much. A book of hours left under a stone. But the rhythm is in it — every psalm, every antiphon, every hour marked and named. If the girl keeps it. If she reads it. If she teaches her children when to pause in the day and breathe —

He does not finish the thought. It is too much to place on a book and a girl and a stone.

He goes back inside.

---

Dawn.

The bell does not ring.

Anselm wakes in the dorter with the other brothers, and for a moment the silence is ordinary — the Great Silence of the night, which will break when the bell calls them to Lauds, as it has broken every morning of his life in this house.

But the bell does not ring.

The prior has given no instruction. Perhaps he could not bring himself to say it: *Do not ring the bell. It belongs to the King now.* Perhaps he simply lay in his cell and let the hour pass, as a man lets a wave pass over him when he is too tired to swim.

The monks stir. No one rises. No one speaks. The Great Silence holds — but it is a different silence now. Not the silence of rest before prayer. Not the silence of the desert fathers, chosen and luminous. This is the silence of absence. Of a sound that should be there and is not. The shape of the bell still hangs in the air, the way a limb aches after it is taken.

Anselm lies still. Through the narrow window, the sky is the grey of old pewter, and somewhere in the village a cock crows, and a dog barks, and life continues in its ordinary way, unaware that anything has ended.

He thinks of the horologium under the stone. He thinks of Agnes's daughter, her quick hands turning pages she half-understands. He thinks of the rhythm — Lauds, Prime, Terce, Sext, None, Vespers, Compline — carried now in a book instead of a bell, in a girl instead of a house.

It is not enough.

It will have to be.

He rises, dresses, and walks to the kitchen to see about the bread. The Commissioners will be hungry when they arrive, and he is, after all, a practical man.
