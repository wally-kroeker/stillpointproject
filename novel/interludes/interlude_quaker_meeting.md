ERA: 17 July 1658 · Colonial Pennsylvania (Lenape land)
LOCATION: Friends’ Meeting-House near Chester River
POV CHARACTER(S): Mary Boulton (young seeker)
VOICE/TENSE: V-Interlude

---

The door-latch falls shut with a wooden sigh. Twenty-three Friends settle on plain benches—their straw hats, lace bonnets, travel dust—until only creak of floorboards and river cicadas remain.

Morning sun slants through unglazed windows, stippling rough-sawn walls. Mary Boulton lowers herself, hands folded, pulse still rushing from the ride. In silence they wait, listening for “that of God” to stir within.

Minutes lengthen. A farm dog yawns beneath the trestle-table. Dust motes drift like galaxies. Somewhere beyond the cornfields, a woodpecker knocks—steady, slower than Mary’s heart. She follows the rhythm, breath softening, the week’s burdens loosening their grip: her brother’s fever, looming debts, news of wars across the sea.

A hush thickens—the famous “gathered” silence Friends speak of. It feels alive, coiling through ribs and rafters alike, as if the entire room inhales once, then holds. Mary realises no one here is leading it; stillness is leading them.

Five, ten, perhaps twenty minutes slide past. Time is unmeasured. Then an elderly man rises. He says nothing at first—only stands within the hush, tears bright in his lashes. When he finally speaks, the words are spare:

“Be not weary. The Light is always at work—even beneath sorrow’s ground.”

He sits. Silence returns, thicker, tender. Something inside Mary unlatches—an ache and a relief at once—yet no one moves to soothe her; the hush itself does the mending.

At last, three Friends feel a simultaneous release and open their eyes. The gathered meeting breaks like dawn after eclipse: slow smiles, cider shared from a clay pitcher, gentle inquiry about each soul’s well-being. But no one analyses the silence. They carry it, wordless, into horse-carts and fields—seed for the week to come.

Mary mounts her mare an hour later, the Light still humming in her chest, and thinks: If the world paused like this more often, wars might forget their purpose.

She rides toward the river, heart quiet as unstruck bell metal.
