ERA: Late Summer 1892 · Diné (Navajo) Territory, Mesa east of Canyon de Chelly
LOCATION: “Big Water Hogan,” temporary ceremonial lodge beside a sandstone rim
POV CHARACTER(S): Ashkii Yazzie (15-year-old initiate) & Hatałii Benay
VOICE/TENSE: V-Interlude

---

Night still grips the high desert, a velvet bowl pricked with the last shards of the Pleiades. Inside the brush-roof hogan, juniper embers pulse like slow hearts, scenting the air with resin and memory. The Night Chant—Tłʼééjí—has wound through eight evenings already. Songs braided with rattle and drum have worked their medicine around the feverish child lying on a sheep-wool pallet. Tonight is the ninth, the closing. Dawn must arrive balanced, or the healing will drift away like dust on morning wind.

Fifteen-year-old Ashkii Yazzie kneels near the eastern doorway, tasked with feeding the ember bed. His fingers smell of piñon pitch; his stomach jitters with sleeplessness and the privilege of being allowed so close to the rite. Beyond the door’s cloth flap, the mesa edge falls into a hush so deep it seems to drink the stars.

At the circle’s center, Hatałii Benay—the singer—unwraps a roll of cotton. Pigments glimmer within: crimson earth, white ash, yellow sandstone, cedar-green dust. He has three hours before first light to call the Holy Ones into form on the hogan floor, then erase them again before the world wakes.

### The Painting of Dawn

Benay’s voice, low as arroyo water at night, speaks only to the earth:
“Shá bikʼehgo éí nihá,” For our footsteps to be lucky.

With two fingers he scatters red sand in an arc—north to east. Ashkii watches, breath held. Each color lands without plume or waste, like snowflakes guided by unseen lines. Shapes emerge: a pair of feathered guardians, lightning twisted into wings, the Rainbow People arching protectively around them. The design is older than the canyon walls, mapped in Benay’s memory the way sheep know hidden springs.

Ashkii’s thoughts flutter: railroad whistles to the west, government men talking of boarding schools, coughs that took his younger sister last winter. The singer pauses, looks up, pupils reflecting ember-gold. “Grandson,” he murmurs, “mind that walks ahead of breath is a dog chasing wind.”

Ashkii flushes, nods, feels his ribs loosen. He matches his inhalation to Benay’s arm strokes—slow, sure, no hurry in the universe.

### Inside the Chant

The rattle begins—gourd filled with obsidian chips—syncopated with a goatskin drum. Four men and two women join, their vocals weaving a canyon of sound inside the hogan: a call to the Night Gods, to black-disked skies, to thunder’s horse. The sick child whimpers, then settles, lulled by vibrations that seem to stroke the fever itself like a restless colt.

Ashkii tends the fire. Each log crack gives off sparks that wheel upward, briefly mirroring constellations outside. He realises the ceremony is a universe in miniature: floor for earth, roof poles for sky, sand-beings for the powers between. He, the fire-keeper, is only one turning grain.

Benay’s chant swells—rising at the edges of human throat then falling into shared silence so sudden Ashkii can hear juniper sap pop. In those pauses the stillness feels alive, a presence sitting among them, listening with impossible patience.

### The Moment of Gaze

When the painting is complete, Benay beckons the child’s mother. She lifts her son, guiding his fever-bright eyes over each sacred figure—north, west, south, east—so the Holy Ones may imprint harmony on his mind. Ashkii feels gooseflesh: the child’s gaze seems to draw light from the sand itself; colors brighten, edges quiver. He glimpses, for half a heartbeat, what the old stories mean when they say pictures breathe.

Then, softly, Benay covers the design with blue cornmeal. A sweep of an eagle feather, and the sand is just earth again. Impermanence is the seal of power: beauty must fall away so the pattern can live inside the people.

### Erasure & Hush

With first hint of grey in the east, singers hush. Benay’s palm lifts—silence absolute. Even the mesquite outside stills as if listening. Ashkii notices his own pulse has slowed to match the dying ember glow. In that hush a truth blooms, scentless and bright: the world balances itself when people remember to stop stirring the dust.

Benay breaks the spell by grinding the spent pigments into a small leather pouch. “Offer these to the river bend at midday,” he tells Ashkii. “Water carries the last prayers to places we can’t walk.”

Ashkii bows, accepting the pouch. It weighs almost nothing, yet feels heavier than iron with meaning.

### Dawn-Balance

They step outside. Daybreak ignites the mesa rim, sandstone walls flushing rose, then gold. A bluebird flickers across the light, as if testing new brushstrokes on the raw canvas of morning. The fevered boy is carried out; his skin is cool, breathing even. His mother’s sob catches in her throat—half laugh, half prayer.

Benay faces east, arms open, the “Dawn-Balance” posture. Ashkii mirrors him, cornmeal pouch clutched to his chest. Wind slides between them, neither cold nor warm—perfect middle breath of the world.

“Walk in beauty,” Benay intones, the final words of Night Chant. “Beauty before you, beauty behind, beauty above, beauty below, beauty within.”

Ashkii repeats, voice cracking but certain. The chant ends, yet the stillness persists, thicker than canyon shadow at noon. He senses this hush is older than songs, older than clans, older even than the river that cuts this land. It is the original medicine—always offered, rarely tasted.

### Epilogue Sigh

Later, when he rides a shaggy pony toward the river bend, the sun climbs high, heat waves skitter off basalt. But inside Ashkii, the hush moves with each heartbeat—portable, unbreakable. He understands now why the sandpainting had to be erased: the pattern lives on in breath and gait, in how he will greet strangers, in how he will someday sing over another sick child.

He tips the pouch into the water; colored grains swirl, then vanish. The river shines back a sky so still it seems newly made. Ashkii smiles, tasting the sweetness of silence on his tongue, and turns homeward, balanced.
