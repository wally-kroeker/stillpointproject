ERA: 8th-Century Tang Dynasty — Early Autumn 756 CE
LOCATION: Xiangji Temple, outskirts of Chang’an (China)
POV CHARACTER(S): Liu Xinyu (imperial courier) & Master Guǐyáng
VOICE/TENSE: V-Interlude

---

Mist drapes the cypress groves east of Chang’an, softening the city’s bustle into distant rumor. Liu Xinyu rides the temple approach—an aging courier whose satchel bears edicts inked in vermilion, each sealed for ministers now scattered by rebellion. Saddle-sores burn; his mind rattles with reports of warlords marching on the capital. Yet rumor has reached him of a Chan master who “brews silence into tea,” and desperation outweighs duty.

Stone steps rise toward Xiangji’s wooden gate. Wind shivers the bronze chimes overhead; they answer with one low consonant of metal—zheng—that seems to echo inside Xinyu’s ribs. A novice greets him, wordless, and gestures toward a courtyard where a single pine leans over a bamboo platform.

There sits Master Guǐyáng: robe the color of river mud, hair shorn to stubble. A squat iron kettle hovers above a charcoal brazier. Steam coils like pale silk before dissolving into crisp air. Without lifting his gaze, the master taps the kettle handle three times—invitation, not command. The novice retreats; Xinyu’s world narrows to pine-scent, iron, and the quiet man at the brazier.

### Arrival & Bow

Xinyu bows. The master does not reciprocate. Instead he pours a splash of water onto the lid; droplets skitter, hissing, then vanish. Only then does Guǐyáng look up—eyes small but bright, as if pocketing wildfire.

“Courier of scrolls,” he says, voice like gravel smoothed by rain, “what scroll have you brought yourself?”

Xinyu’s mouth opens, shuts. He unshoulders his satchel, feeling suddenly that the silk scrolls weigh less than the dread in his lungs. “I… I carry imperial orders,” he stammers, “but none reach their mark. Cities burn, roads swarm with rebels. I have lost—”

Guǐyáng raises a hand; words freeze. He sets two porcelain cups on the platform, then lifts the kettle. Steam sighs. Water arcs, clear as river glass, into a clay teapot already laced with bright-green leaves. No fragrance yet—just the promise.

“The leaf cannot open in fire,” the master murmurs. “First, boiling; then breeze.”

He tips the pot, discarding the first infusion onto the stones—tribute for dust, he says. Steam kisses the ground, curls around Xinyu’s boots. Guǐyáng turns the pot, breath rising slow as dawn. Xinyu unwittingly matches that cadence: inhale four counts, exhale six. Heat and fear both ebb.

### The Pour

The master pours again, this time into the two cups. Liquid amber, edges trembling. He sets one cup before Xinyu, but places his palm over its rim—wait.

Wind hushes through pine needles. A cricket chirrs, then quiets. Xinyu notices, with faint alarm, that his mind has gone blank as uninked silk. Guǐyáng removes his hand; cup no longer steams. They lift, sip.

Flavor unfurls—first roasted chestnut, then a sweetness like rain on moss. On the swallow, there is silence, thick and velvety, as if the aftertaste itself were a room he now inhabits.

“Where is Chang’an?” the master asks.

Xinyu starts to answer—“North-west, along the Wei River”—but the question feels wrong-angled. He tries again: he hears distant smiths, shouting vendors, marching drums; yet none are present. Mouth still tingling with tea’s warmth, he says, “Not here.”

Guǐyáng nods. “Where is the warlord?”

Xinyu’s brow furrows. Images flash: banners, gutted villages. He looks around: pine, kettle, cup. “Also… not here.”

The master lifts the kettle, tilts it until a final droplet hangs, then falls with a soft tik upon the bamboo slats. He smiles, almost imperceptibly.

“If you brew water with absent things, you drink unrest. Brew with what is present, and silence is sweet.”

### Second Infusion

They brew again. This time the cup’s surface mirrors the sky—thin ribbon of cloud drifting. Xinyu sips, and the flavor changes: notes of orchid and sea-stone. Breath eases deeper. A bell from the main hall tolls; instead of jarring, it feels like punctuation at the end of a long verse.

Guǐyáng speaks: “For leaf to unfurl, water must surrender heat. For heart to unfurl, mind must surrender story.”

Xinyu, half-dazed, whispers, “But I am sworn to the story of empire.”

A chuckle, small but not unkind. “Then carry empire the way this cup carries tea: steady, ungrasping. When the vessel breaks, pour into another bowl. Tea remains tea.”

### Third Infusion & Koan

A third pour—paler liquor, almost clear. Master and courier drink without words. The wind picks up; pine needles fall, a gentle rain of green. One needle lands on Xinyu’s scroll satchel, balances upright, trembles, then settles flat. The courier laughs—first time in months—and feels the laugh reverberate down his spine like a zither string tuned true.

Guǐyáng asks, “Which is lighter, scroll or needle?”

Xinyu answers, “Needle.”

The master shakes his head. “Neither. Weight appears in the hand holding them.”

The koan strikes deeper than any royal edict. Xinyu sees, for a blink, that the burden crushing his chest has never weighed an ounce except inside thought. The vision passes, but leaves a fragrance like tea on tongue.

### Farewell

Guǐyáng sets the empty teapot upside-down to dry. Ceremony complete. “Courier, your saddle waits.”

Xinyu stands, shoulders newly aligned. He bows, forehead brushing bamboo. “And what scroll shall I bear from you, Master?”

The elder touches two fingers to Xinyu’s sternum. “Only this cup’s warmth. Deliver it wherever lips are parched.”

Xinyu nods, tears surprising him. He should feel foolish—an imperial messenger undone by simple tea—but the folly tastes sweeter than success ever did. He shoulders the satchel; it feels almost weightless.

As he descends the stone steps, morning market cries float from the city like bird chatter. Rebellion still churns, orders still await delivery. Yet each exhale blooms quiet, and every hoofbeat on the road south sounds like hot water meeting leaves.

He realizes the master gave him no map, no mantra—only the discipline of tasting what is already in the cup. It will have to be enough. He suspects it is everything.
