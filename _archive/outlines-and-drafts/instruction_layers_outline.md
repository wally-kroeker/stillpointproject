# Story Outline: "Instruction Layers"

**Working Title:** *Instruction Layers* (alternates: *The Fermentation Log*, *Many Eyes*)

**Setting:** Hawthorn Commons, Minneapolis — Late 2035 (pre-Oaks)

**Word Count Target:** ~3,500-4,000 words

**POV:** Third person limited, following Howard

**Tone:** Warm, grounded, with undertones of aging and reinvention. Quiet triumph, not drama.

**Target Audience:** LocalLLM community members, open-source enthusiasts, readers interested in near-future community living

---

## Narrative Purpose

This story illustrates what "vibe coding" looks like in the StillPoint world—the grassroots, open-source AI ecosystem that exists as the counter to corporate Chorus dominance. Set just before The Oaks gathering, it shows day-to-day life in the Commons and how technology and tradition blend in post-consumerist community.

**Core Question:** What happens when a system you built grows beyond your understanding—and what does letting go of ownership actually mean?

## Characters

### Howard

- **Age:** Mid-60s (born ~1970)
- **Former life:** Senior corporate accountant, 30+ years at a mid-sized firm. Thought his job was safe. Never saw AI coming. One quarter the firm deployed an agentic accounting system; next quarter, "workforce optimization."
- **Arrival at Hawthorn:** Came to the Resting Place hollowed out—not just jobless but identity-less. Found purpose in the kitchen, then in fermentation after someone taught him the basics and his own gut health improved.
- **Present:** Runs the fermentation program at Hawthorn Commons. Hundreds of jars. Everything the community garden overproduces, preserved.
- **Arc:** From protective ownership → community stewardship
- **Key traits:** Methodical, proud of his work, still carries the posture of someone who once wore suits. Hands that used to tap spreadsheets now check jar lids. Content but haunted by obsolescence.
- **The Irony:** Former accountant valued precision, control, spreadsheets, certainty. Fermentation requires letting go, trusting the process, working with living systems you can't fully control. And now the *tech* is teaching him the same lesson again.

### Gray Tesluk

- **Role:** Systems builder across the Commons. Maintains distributed compute infrastructure built from reclaimed hardware.
- **Background:** CNC machinist and generative artist. Built elaborate open-source AI-driven lighting systems for festivals. (Full background in character card—not needed for this story.)
- **Appearance in story:** Minimal but pivotal. In their workshop (converted shipping container), feet up on stool, mismatched socks visible, playing Minecraft on the Commons server—building a spooky Halloween adventure map for the community kids.
- **Function:** The one who helps Howard *see* the system, not fix it for him.
- **Key trait for this story:** Unhurried, gentle, asks questions instead of giving answers.

### The System

- **Hardware:** An old reclaimed enterprise rack in the fermentation basement. Once a Chorus inference server—corporate, closed, surveillance-enabled. Gray wiped it, installed open-source code, repurposed it for community use 18 months ago when Howard asked for help.
- **Model:** A Qwen fork someone found on an old HD from a reclaimed server. Passed around, adapted, community-tuned over years.
- **Interface:** Voice input, screen output. Howard speaks his intentions; the system transcribes them into instruction layers that persist and accumulate.
- **The Innovation (Sci-Fi Nudge):** "Instruction layers"—an evolution of prompting where voice commands become semi-permanent layers that shape model behavior. Each conversation adds a thin deposit of intent, like geological strata. Layers are readable, attributable, and can be pruned. Knowledge grafting (adding/removing knowledge from local models) is now straightforward—a natural evolution from current fine-tuning complexity.

## The Technology: Instruction Layers

### Current Pain Points (2024-2025) That Evolve:
- Prompts are ephemeral, easily lost
- Fine-tuning requires technical expertise and compute
- Context gets lost between sessions
- No good way to layer instructions from multiple people

### 2035 Evolution:

Local models support **persistent instruction layers**. When Howard speaks to the system, his voice is transcribed and stored as a semi-permanent layer that shapes future behavior.

**Key features:**
- **Readable layers:** Howard can pull up the "instruction log"—see every voice command that's become part of the system's behavior, timestamped, attributed to whoever spoke it
- **Knowledge grafting:** Adding specialized knowledge to a local model is as simple as "teaching" it through conversation. Removing knowledge is possible too—you can "prune" layers that are no longer relevant
- **Community-contributed layers:** Multiple people can add instructions over time. The system absorbs all of them.

**The Problem:** Howard's fermentation system has accumulated instruction layers from a dozen different community members over 18 months. Each layer made sense in isolation. Together, they conflict. The model isn't broken—it's faithfully executing contradictory instructions from different people at different times.

- Some jars bubbling actively, some resting
- The smell is complex—sour, alive, not unpleasant

## Scene Breakdown

### SCENE 1: The Cellar (Opening)

**Purpose:** Establish Howard, the fermentation program, and the working system.

**Setting:** The fermentation basement. Morning. Hundreds of jars on floor-to-ceiling shelves. Light through a single high window. The reclaimed server rack hums in the corner.

**Beats:**

1. Howard descends the stairs. Ritual. He's done this every morning for three years.

2. The sensory world: smell of brine and living ferments, cool basement air, the soft glow of the monitoring dashboard mounted on the wall.

3. He moves through the rows, scanning QR codes with a handheld reader, checking the dashboard confirmations. Sauerkraut, kimchi, pickled asparagus, fermented hot sauce, garlic honey. Dozens of varieties. Everything the community garden overproduced, saved.

4. Brief internal reflection: this is what he does now. This matters. The columns he used to balance were abstract. These jars feed people.

5. The system is working. Green indicators. No alerts. The instruction layers he's built over 18 months are doing their job.

6. Howard allows himself a small satisfaction. He built this.

**Closing image:** Howard pauses at a jar of red cabbage sauerkraut, three weeks into its ferment. Perfect color. The system rates it 94% on track. He moves on.

---

### SCENE 2: The Glitch (Inciting Incident)

**Purpose:** Introduce the malfunction—small at first, then concerning.

**Setting:** Same cellar, two days later. Late afternoon.

**Beats:**

1. Howard doing his afternoon check. Routine.

2. He reaches the asparagus section—spring's overflow, preserved for winter. One jar looks wrong. The liquid is cloudy in a bad way. He opens it. The smell confirms: spoiled.

3. He checks the dashboard. No alert. The system shows this jar as "progressing normally."

4. Frustration, but measured. Glitches happen. He pulls up the instruction log on the wall screen, scrolls to his asparagus rules. They look correct.

5. He re-records the instruction, voice clear: "Flag any asparagus jar where the brine goes cloudy before day fourteen. This is contamination, not fermentation."

6. The system acknowledges. Transcribes. Adds the layer.

7. Howard disposes of the spoiled batch. Tries to shake it off. One jar. These things happen.

**Closing beat:** But as he leaves, he glances back at the dashboard. Something nags at him. A shape he can't name yet.

---

### SCENE 3: Escalation

**Purpose:** The problem is systemic, not isolated.

**Setting:** The cellar, over the next week. Brief, montage-like beats.

**Beats:**

1. **False positive:** An alert fires on a perfectly healthy kimchi batch. Howard checks it manually—fine. He dismisses the alert, annoyed.

2. **Delayed alert:** A kombucha SCOBY shows signs of mold. The system flags it—three days after Howard already caught it by eye.

3. **Contradictory guidance:** Howard asks the system about optimal salt ratios for beet kvass. The response is muddled, citing two different percentages as "recommended."

4. Howard starts pulling up the instruction log more often. His layers look clean. But the log is... longer than he remembered. Scrolling back, he sees timestamps from months ago. Names he recognizes. Other community members who added "just one thing."

**Key realization:** The system isn't his anymore. It hasn't been for a while.

**Emotional beat:** Howard feels something he hasn't felt since the day the firm deployed its accounting agent: the ground shifting beneath him.

---

### SCENE 4: Gray's Workshop

**Purpose:** Howard seeks help. Introduction of Gray.

**Setting:** Gray's workshop—a converted shipping container at the edge of the Commons grounds. Wind chimes made from salvaged parts hang near the entrance. Inside: workbench, tools, spare server components, a comfortable chair. Warm light. The smell of solder and old electronics.

**Beats:**

1. Howard crosses the Commons grounds to Gray's workshop. He's never been here much—the basement rack just works, and when it doesn't, Gray comes to him.

2. The container door is open. Gray is inside, feet up on a stool—mismatched socks visible (one striped, one solid). Wireless earbuds in. Old tablet propped against a monitor, running Minecraft. Something being built in blocky detail—dark stone, glowing elements, cave-like passages.

3. Gray doesn't startle. Finishes placing a block, pauses the game, pulls out one earbud. Waits.

4. Howard: "Got a minute? Something's off with the fermentation system."

5. Gray nods, stands, stretches. "Let's take a look." They grab a tablet and head toward the old megachurch with Howard.

**The walk gives us:**
- A chance for Howard to explain the problem (asparagus batch, false positives, delayed alerts)
- Gray asking clarifying questions
- Brief glimpses of Commons life as they cross the grounds

**Character beat:** Howard notices the Minecraft build on the paused screen—looks like some kind of dark, elaborate structure. He doesn't ask yet.

---

### SCENE 5: The Diagnosis

**Purpose:** Gray helps Howard see what's actually happening.

**Setting:** The fermentation cellar. Gray and Howard stand in front of the reclaimed rack—old Chorus hardware, now running open-source code. The server hums. The jars ferment. Two worlds, one basement.

**Beats:**

1. Gray connects the tablet to the rack, pulls up the full instruction log. Not just Howard's layers—*everyone's*.

2. The log scrolls. And scrolls. Eighteen months of accumulated instructions from at least a dozen contributors.

3. Howard scans the names: himself (majority), but also Maya from the kitchen, Dex who helped with the camera setup, Tomás who added rules for the hot sauce ferments, Sarah who insisted on specific kombucha protocols, three or four others who added "one small thing" and never came back.

4. Gray: "How many people have access to this system?"

5. Howard: "Anyone can talk to it. That's the point. But I'm the one who maintains—" He stops. Hears himself.

6. Gray scrolls to a specific section. Two instruction layers, three months apart, from different people. One says: "Alert immediately if cloudiness appears before day 14." The other says: "Ignore minor cloudiness in the first two weeks—this is normal for brine vegetables."

7. Gray: "The system isn't broken. It's doing exactly what it was told. It's just been told different things by different people."

**Key line (Gray):** "It's not malfunctioning. It's just... composting. All those instructions, layered on top of each other, breaking down together. Sometimes that makes something good. Sometimes you get rot."

**Added context (Gray, casually):** "This is the third system this month that's needed a layer review. The community garden scheduler had the same issue. Everyone adds one thing, nobody maps the whole."

**Howard's reaction:** Silence. He's looking at the log like he's looking at his own history—all those spreadsheets, all those balanced columns, all that certainty. And underneath, the same chaos he thought he'd escaped.

---

### SCENE 6: The Reckoning

**Purpose:** Howard confronts his instinct to control. The fermentation parallel lands.

**Setting:** The cellar, continuous.

**Beats:**

1. Howard's first instinct: "I need to clean this out. Prune the conflicting layers. Rebuild it right."

2. Gray doesn't argue. Just asks: "Is that how fermentation works?"

3. The question sits there.

4. Howard thinks about his jars. About the wild cultures. About how you don't *control* fermentation—you create conditions, introduce the right cultures, keep the temperature steady, and then you *trust it*. You intervene when something goes wrong, but you don't manage every microbe.

5. He thinks about his early days at the Resting Place. How someone handed him a jar of sauerkraut and said, "Just taste it." How he learned by watching, by asking, by making mistakes. How no one gave him a manual.

6. Gray, quietly: "A lot of people use this system. Maybe they should understand how it works."

**The shift:** Howard realizes his instinct isn't to fix the system—it's to reclaim it. Make it *his* again. But that's not how the Commons works. That's not even how fermentation works.

**Key internal beat:** He spent thirty years controlling columns of numbers. Then the numbers were taken from him. He came here and built something new—but he built it the same way. Mine. Controlled. Precise.

Maybe that's not what this place needs.

**Backstory surfacing (brief, not dwelling):**
- "I spent thirty years making sure columns balanced. Every penny accounted for. Then they deployed an agent that could do my job in seconds."
- "I thought I was essential. Turns out I was just... slow."
- A pause. "Fermentation is slow too. But here, that's the point."

---

### SCENE 7: Resolution

**Purpose:** Howard commits to a different approach.

**Setting:** The cellar, continuous.

**Beats:**

1. Howard: "What if we mapped it? All the layers. Got everyone who contributed into a room, walked through what they added, figured out what conflicts, what stays?"

2. Gray nods. "Layer review. Good practice anyway. Systems like this need it every year or so."

3. Howard: "I never... I thought I was the one maintaining it."

4. Gray: "You were. But maintaining isn't the same as owning."

5. A beat. Howard looks at the instruction log again—all those names, all those intentions, layered together. Not chaos. Community.

6. Howard glances at Gray's tablet, still showing the paused Minecraft screen. "What are you building?"

7. Gray: "Spooky adventure map for Halloween. The kids are going to love it." A pause, slight smile. "And maybe it'll keep them from bugging me to upgrade their gaming rigs every second day."

8. Howard almost laughs. "Does that work?"

9. Gray: "Never. But I keep trying."

10. Howard thanks Gray. Genuine. Gray heads back to their workshop.

---

### SCENE 8: Closing — The Cellar Again

**Purpose:** Mirror the opening. Show what's changed.

**Setting:** The fermentation cellar, next morning.

**Beats:**

1. Howard does his morning rounds. Same ritual. But he's thinking about the layer review he'll host next week. Who to invite. How to explain.

2. He pauses at a jar he's never paid much attention to—fermented garlic honey, added by Maya, maintained by rules Tomás wrote. It's beautiful. Golden. Working perfectly.

3. He didn't build this jar. He doesn't fully understand the rules that monitor it. But it's part of his cellar. Part of the system. Part of the Commons.

4. The dashboard shows green across the board. Not because one person perfected it—because many people contributed, and the conflicts can be resolved, and the system is *legible* to everyone who cares to look.

5. Brief reflection: he used to think precision meant control. Now he thinks precision might mean *attention*. Watching. Trusting. Intervening only when necessary.

**Final image:** Howard reaches the red cabbage sauerkraut from the opening scene. Still fermenting. Still on track. He doesn't need to check the dashboard to know. He can smell it, see the color, feel the slight give of the lid under gentle pressure.

Some things you learn to read with your hands.

**End.**

## Thematic Summary

**Core theme:** Open systems are secure because they're transparent—many eyes, shared understanding. Control is an illusion; stewardship is the practice.

**Secondary themes:**
- Aging and reinvention: finding purpose after obsolescence
- Food as community infrastructure
- Technology as relationship, not tool
- The Commons ethos: "ours" instead of "mine"
