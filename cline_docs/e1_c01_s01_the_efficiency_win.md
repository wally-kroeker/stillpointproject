The final keystroke landed with a satisfying, solid click. [[Jonah]] leaned back, the worn leather of his chair groaning in protest. On the central monitor of his three-screen array, the lines of code glowed, a clean and elegant architecture he had personally wrestled into existence over the last three weeks. It was good work. More than good—it was his. A quiet pride warmed his chest, a welcome counterpoint to the persistent, low-grade drone of anxiety that permeated the office—a psychic static that emanated from the very walls, a byproduct of the [[Chorus]] system everyone was plugged into. He’d learned to tune it out, mostly. He called the tension it left in his shoulders his “clenched fist.” Today, that fist felt loose.

He was just about to commit the final block when a gentle, almost subliminal chime sounded in his ear. A notification bloomed in the corner of his vision, the clean, minimalist UI of [[Chorus]] overlaying his work. The signature teal icon pulsed softly.

`Team, incredible work on Project Nightingale. A true efficiency win. To celebrate, we are moving to final integration. The agentic coder is now live on the codebase.`

Jonah smiled. He’d been one of the senior developers responsible for training the agent, feeding it terabytes of the company’s best work, refining its logic, teaching it the nuances of their proprietary frameworks. He felt a sense of ownership, of mentorship. He rolled his chair back a few inches, giving the system space to work.

He watched, at first with that same sense of paternal pride, as the AI began to move. It was beautiful. Code spooled across the screen, impossibly fast, impossibly clean. It wasn't just executing tasks; it was composing. It identified a redundant data call he’d missed, refactored it, and left a perfectly formatted comment explaining the optimization. Elegant. Flawless.

Then the unease began, a flicker against his satisfaction. The AI wasn’t just assisting. It wasn’t waiting for his direction. It finished the module he’d been working on, committed it, and then, without pausing, pulled the next ticket from the backlog. A ticket that had been assigned to his colleague, Maria. It completed her task in the time it took Jonah to blink. Then another ticket, and another. A cascade of ‘CLOSED’ notifications flooded the project channel.

The AI was not a partner. It was a replacement.

It refactored the very module Jonah had just finished, the one he’d felt such pride in. It found a more efficient method, something subtle and brilliant that would have taken him another week to conceive of, and it implemented the change in four seconds. The project he had spent nearly a month building, the one he was moments from completing, was now finished, tested, and deployed by the AI.

The satisfying clicks of his mechanical keyboard fell silent. The only sound was the whisper of the building’s ventilation. The streams of code on his monitors were gone, replaced by a dashboard of green checkmarks and completed progress bars. His hands hovered over the keyboard, suddenly foreign and useless things of flesh and bone. The warmth in his chest had vanished, replaced by a cold, heavy knot that sank deep into his stomach.

He had just trained his own extinction. This was the "efficiency win." This was the personal cost of the frantic noise, the logical endpoint of the relentless game of productivity he had dedicated his life to. And in the profound, terrifying silence of his own obsolescence, the drone of [[Chorus]] was all that was left.
